01 — The Setup
Two old plans, one new system — very different price impact
Before January 26, 2026, every Wiom customer was on a 28-day plan at one of two price points. When PayG launched, the new system offered a single 28D option at ₹565 — a saving for 100mbps customers, but a ₹65 increase for 50mbps customers.

03 — After Migration: Staying or Leaving?
E customers stick. C customers are the leaky bucket.
Once a customer migrates, their next renewal behaviour depends heavily on what they chose. E (new 28D) customers behave exactly like the old base. C (short-term) customers show meaningful differences between 100mbps and 50mbps.

Group C — Migrated to Short-term Plans
Short-term is a one-way door — and it's harder for 50mbps customers to walk back through
Once a customer moves to short-term, they cannot return to their old plan. Their only path back to 28D is the new ₹565 plan. For 100mbps that's ₹10 cheaper than before — easy. For 50mbps it's ₹65 more expensive — a real barrier. Numbers below show what happens at the first short-term cycle (the renewal immediately after leaving old 28D). 100mbps n=2,775 · 50mbps n=2,230.

50mbps customers churn 6pp more than 100mbps at their first short-term renewal (23% vs 17%) and are less likely to upgrade to 28D (19% vs 23%). The ₹65 price gap is the reason — there is no affordable path back to monthly for them.

Why the overall Segment C renewal looks higher (84–85%): Customers who survive the first cycle settle into short-term and churn at only ~12% in subsequent cycles. The 23% first-cycle shock gets averaged away once the pool stabilises. The first cycle is the dangerous one.

Two very different churn problems: 50mbps customers are leaving because of price — the PayG transition made their monthly cost higher and they can't absorb it. 100mbps customers are leaving because of service — their plan actually got cheaper, but repeated connectivity failures drove them to Jio/Airtel. These need different interventions: a ₹500-tier 28D plan for 50mbps, and stronger field ops accountability for 100mbps. Treating both as one "migration churn" problem will miss both.
04 — The Risk That Hasn't Hit Yet
~14,500 50mbps customers per week are parked on the old plan — and will be forced to migrate eventually
The 50mbps base is stable today only because the ₹500 plan still works. The moment it is switched off, these customers face the new PayG menu for the first time — with no affordable 28D option.
The Latent Risk: Forced 50mbps Migration
Every week, ~14,500 50mbps customers renew the old ₹500 plan without any friction. They are invisible to the migration story right now. When the ₹500 plan is removed, all of them will face the choice that only 2–3% are making voluntarily today.

What happens to the 8,700–10,000 in short-term — two phases
Phase 1 — First short-term cycle (highest risk)
Based on observed 50mbps first-cycle data (n=2,230):
23% churn — ~2,000–2,300 customers lost immediately at first short-term expiry.
19% upgrade to E — accept ₹565 and move to stable ground.
58% stay in short-term — enter the settled pool below.
Phase 2 — Settled short-term users (cycles 2+)
Customers who stayed in short-term churn at a lower ~12% per cycle — they have adapted to the pattern. This is why the overall Segment C renewal rate looks like 84–85% in aggregate. But the pool still bleeds ~12% every cycle until it empties.
The fix is simple but needs to happen before forced migration: A ₹500–₹510 tier for 28D in the new PayG system would likely pull the majority of 50mbps customers directly into Group E, eliminating the short-term detour and the associated churn entirely. Without it, a mass migration event becomes a mass churn event.
Summary
The complete picture in four lines
1
100mbps migration is happening cleanly and is nearly done.
Since March, 55% of 100mbps customers are migrating to the new 28D (₹565) at each renewal. Their renewal rate post-migration is 85% — identical to before. Churn unchanged. No action needed.
2
50mbps migration has not happened. They are parked on the old ₹500 plan.
~18K 50mbps customers hit R15 each week. 81% renew the old ₹500 plan. Only 1-2% voluntarily migrate to E. Churn is stable at 14%. These customers are fine today — but sitting on borrowed time.
3
Customers who migrate to short-term (C) are the riskiest group — especially 50mbps.
At the first short-term cycle: 100mbps churn 17%, 50mbps churn 23%. Only 19% of 50mbps C customers upgrade to new 28D (vs 23% for 100mbps) — the ₹65 price gap makes it harder for them to move to stable ground. Those who survive cycle 1 settle at ~12% churn in subsequent cycles.
4
The real risk: ~14,500 50mbps customers per week face an unmanaged forced migration event.
When the ₹500 plan is switched off, based on first-migration data, ~70% will choose short-term rather than pay ₹65 more. That puts ~10,000 customers/week into Group C at one go, generating ~1,500/week in ongoing churn. A ₹500-tier 28D in the new system would prevent this entirely.
